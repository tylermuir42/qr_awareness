// 02 — Live Demonstration

Experience the risk (by choice)

QR codes can trigger links and downloads that waste bandwidth, fill storage, or worse. This demo intentionally downloads a ~300MB file to show how disruptive a “free reward” can be.

Preparing download…

That was the lesson.

Even harmless data can be disruptive at scale. A malicious QR code could just as easily send you to a phishing site, push you toward a scam payment, or download something dangerous.

  • Preview the URL before opening it.
  • Be suspicious of “free rewards” and urgency prompts.
  • Don’t scan codes from unsolicited texts/emails.
  • Look for tampering (stickers over real codes).
// 03 — Stay Safe
🔍

Preview First

Most camera apps show the URL before you open it. Always read it — does the domain look legitimate?

🏧

Check for Stickers

Criminals place rogue QR stickers over real ones. Look for signs of tampering on parking meters, menus, and flyers.

📵

No Urgency = No Scan

Legitimate services don't demand you scan a code immediately or threaten consequences for not scanning.

🔒

HTTPS Isn't Enough

HTTPS means the connection is encrypted — not that the site is safe. Phishing sites use HTTPS too.

🧠

Context Matters

Is a QR code on a random flyer, unsolicited email, or text message? That's a red flag regardless of where it leads.

📲

Use Dedicated Scanners

Some QR scanner apps provide malicious URL detection before you're taken to the page.
